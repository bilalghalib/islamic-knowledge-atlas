1. The Chaney Gap

Eric Chaney (2023) argues that the proportion of scientists in the Islamic world declined after the 11th century. Our reanalysis of 35,606 VIAF records, classified by Claude Sonnet 4.6, finds a consistently higher scientist percentage in every century. The gap widens in precisely the centuries Chaney identifies as the steepest decline.

Our title-based classification (green) vs Chaney's biographical dictionary classification (red). The gap widens from +1.4% in the 1100s to +3.8% in the 1400s.

2. The Domain River: What MENA Scholars Actually Produced

Every field grows in absolute terms through the supposed "decline." Tasawwuf grows 7.2x. Fiqh grows 3.8x. Poetry grows 3.1x. Medicine remains stable. The "decline" is purely proportional: science grew, everything else grew faster.

Absolute counts of MENA scholars by domain, 800s-1400s. No field declines. The tradition reallocated, it did not contract.

3. Commentary: Stagnation or Depth?

Chaney claims commentaries rose from under 10% to over 30%, evidence of "channeling intellectual production towards reproducing approved knowledge." Our data shows commentary as a stable 6-14% of classified works. Saliba (2007) notes these were "in some ways superior to modern periodicals in their demand for comprehensive synthesis."

Commentary percentage by century. Stable, not exploding. Each commentary requires mastery of all previous layers.

7. Methodology

Data Source

85,043 author records from the complete VIAF (Virtual International Authority File) 2024 dump. Filtered to pre-1500 CE authors with work titles. 35,606 successfully classified.

Classification

Each scholar classified by Claude Sonnet 4.6 with two layers: title evidence (strictly from library catalog titles) and background knowledge (from training data, with Wikipedia references). Confidence scored 0-100.

Two Scientist Measures

Strict: title evidence alone supports a science domain with confidence ≥ 60. Broad: any evidence (title or background) supports science with confidence ≥ 40. Chaney's measure uses occupation labels from biographical dictionaries.

Limitations

VIAF overrepresents scholars whose works survived in modern library collections. Classification depends on transliterated titles, which vary across library systems. Background knowledge may introduce post-hoc information not available to contemporaries.
