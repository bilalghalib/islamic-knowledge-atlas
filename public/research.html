A Note on "Research Shows"

There's a powerful psychological effect when someone says "research shows" or "the data indicates." It carries authority. Numbers feel objective in a way that arguments don't. This is precisely why it matters to check what the numbers are actually doing.

When I first read Chaney's paper, I thought: "of course, this confirms what I've always heard — Ghazali broke everything." The numbers seemed to prove it. But when we normalised for population, the "decline" disappeared. When we classified by work titles instead of occupation labels, the scientist count nearly quintupled. The same VIAF database, the same scholars, the same centuries — different frame, different story.

Data is never raw. It arrives pre-shaped by the questions asked, the categories used, and the things not counted. This atlas is offered not as the "real" answer but as a demonstration that the frame matters as much as the content. Use it to ask better questions, not to win arguments.

The Four Essay Options and What This Data Offers Each

Option 1: Modern Subjectivity and the "Psychological Self"

How did the modern transformation of subjectivity reshape understandings of the human person?

What the data shows:

  • The Domain River chart shows that tasawwuf (the science of the soul/self) grew 7.2x through the "decline" centuries. The tradition was INVESTING in understanding the self — just not the buffered, autonomous self that modern psychology studies.
  • The polymath data (5.5% of scholars working across 3+ domains) shows a different model of selfhood: the integrated knower, not the narrow specialist. The kind of self a civilisation produces reflects its anthropology.
  • The commentary tradition (stable at ~11%) embodies a self that receives knowledge through chains of transmission, not a self that produces knowledge from its own certainty.
Potential argument: The modern transformation of subjectivity didn't just change how we understand the person — it changed what we count as knowledge about persons. The Chaney data demonstrates this at civilisational scale: a tradition that invested in the sciences of the soul (tasawwuf, kalam, fiqh) appears to be "declining" when measured by a framework that only counts material sciences.

Option 2: Religion/Spirituality in Modern Psychology

How has modern psychology conceptualised religion and spirituality?

What the data shows:

  • The Chaney Gap chart is itself an example of how modern scholarship conceptualises religion: as something separate from (and opposed to) science. Our classification shows this binary doesn't hold — 6.5% of scholars are scientists by their titles alone, but many of those same scholars also wrote on fiqh, kalam, and tasawwuf.
  • The MENA library bias data shows that commentary works (10% of MENA-catalogued titles) are nearly invisible in Western libraries (1%). The commentary tradition — the primary medium of Islamic intellectual engagement with religion — is structurally underrepresented in the very databases used to study it.
  • The regional data shows 57.7% of classified scholars are European. Islamic intellectual history is studied through Western-dominated catalogues.
Potential argument: Modern psychology's conceptualisation of religion reproduces the same classification error visible in the Chaney data. Just as the science/religion binary erases polymaths who worked across domains, the psychology/spirituality binary reduces rich traditions of soul-formation (tasawwuf, tazkiya) to "religious coping mechanisms." What Freud's "disciplinary inversion" does to revelation (placing it under psychology's jurisdiction), Chaney's framework does to the Islamic sciences (placing them under the science/religion binary).

Option 3: Integration, Indigenisation, and the Islamic Paradigm

Which provides a more robust basis — integration or epistemology-first?

What the data shows:

  • The container error demonstrated at civilisational scale: Chaney "integrated" Islamic intellectual history into Western categories (science/not-science) and produced a finding (decline) that the categories themselves generated. This is structurally identical to mapping shukr onto PERMA's "positive emotion" and losing the root ('ubudiyyah).
  • The Domain River shows what the tradition actually built when operating from its own categories: an integrated knowledge ecology where fiqh, kalam, tasawwuf, medicine, astronomy, and poetry all grew together. No field was sacrificed for another.
  • The population-normalised data shows per-capita intellectual output RISING during civilisational catastrophe (Mongol invasions, Black Death). The theological infrastructure sustained knowledge production when material conditions collapsed. Integration approaches can't explain this; epistemology-first approaches can (the telos transcends circumstance).
Potential argument: Integration inherits the container error. When you place Islamic concepts into Western categories, you reproduce the same mistake Chaney makes: the categories decide what counts before the content arrives. The data shows this isn't just a theoretical problem — it produces measurable distortions (1.4% vs 6.5% scientists). Epistemology-first avoids this because it builds from the tradition's own categories, which organise knowledge by obligation (fard/mandub/mubah) rather than by type (science/not-science).

Option 4: A Comparative Account of Human Flourishing

How has modernity shaped the question of flourishing and the available answers?

What the data shows:

  • The full dataset (85,043 records, 35,606 classified) demonstrates that a civilisation oriented by 'ubudiyyah (servitude to God) produced MORE scholars per capita in its darkest century than in its brightest. That is a form of flourishing that PERMA cannot measure.
  • The Domain River shows the tradition invested in the sciences of human formation (tasawwuf, kalam, fiqh) — not as alternatives to natural science but alongside it. This is flourishing understood as the soul's development, not as the individual's subjective wellbeing.
  • The Chaney Gap shows that how you measure flourishing determines what you find. A civilisation that invests in domains not recognised as "flourishing" within a modern taxonomy appears, within that taxonomy, to be declining.
  • The commentary chain data shows knowledge maintained and deepened across 700 years (the Hikam of Ibn Ata'illah, still studied today at CMC). That is sustainability — a form of flourishing the "more and faster" framework codes as stagnation.
Potential argument: Modernity shaped the flourishing question by narrowing it to what can be measured by the buffered self (subjective wellbeing, self-report scales). The Chaney data shows this narrowing operating at civilisational scale. The Islamic tradition offers a different account: constitutive 'afiya (soundness of orientation toward God) coexists with grief, loss, and civilisational catastrophe. The Prophet wept for Ibrahim but said nothing that displeased Allah. A psychology that can only see the therapeutic kind will systematically misread people and civilisations oriented toward the constitutive kind.

What Each Chart Can Argue

The Chaney Gap (dual line chart)

Argues: Classification method determines the finding. The same scholars, classified by work titles rather than occupation labels, yield a higher scientist count in every century. The gap widens in the "decline" centuries because biographical dictionaries undercount polymaths.

Use it for: Any argument about measurement frameworks, category errors, or the politics of classification.

The Domain River (stacked area)

Argues: No field declines in absolute terms. The tradition reallocated, it did not contract. Tasawwuf (7.2x), fiqh (3.8x), poetry (3.1x), history (3.7x) all grew. Medicine was stable.

Use it for: Reframing "decline" as "reallocation," showing what the tradition actually valued, arguing that the Islamic sciences were not a retreat from knowledge but an expansion of what counted as knowledge.

Commentary Reality (bar chart)

Argues: Commentaries are a stable 6-14% of classified works, not the 30% Chaney claims from Hajji Khalifa. And commentary ≠ stagnation. It's the medium through which knowledge is transmitted, tested, and deepened across generations.

Use it for: Arguments about the commentary tradition, knowledge transmission, isnad, and the difference between maintenance and stagnation.

Regional Distribution (donut)

Argues: 57.7% of VIAF records are European. The database through which we study Islamic intellectual history is dominated by Western library holdings. MENA libraries catalog 13.8% of 800s scholars but only 7.1% of 1400s scholars — later Islamic scholarship is disproportionately preserved by Western institutions.

Use it for: Arguments about epistemic justice, colonial knowledge structures, and why "absence of evidence is not evidence of absence."

Scholar Explorer (filterable cards)

Argues: Polymaths are real, documented, and numerous. Each card links to Wikipedia. Each classification shows evidence. The one-box classification erases people who held knowledge together.

Use it for: Specific examples of polymaths, scientists, or scholars who contradict the decline narrative. Find someone in your century/region of interest and trace their work.

What Would Make This Better

This atlas is a beginning, not an end. Here's what would strengthen it:

  • Full-text access: Link each scholar to their actual works in OpenITI (11,195 texts already digitised) or other digital libraries
  • Cross-referencing: Match our VIAF data against Wikidata (13,600 scholars with structured occupations) and Open Library (13,466 scholars with work counts)
  • Manuscript databases: Integrate data from FIHRIST (UK Islamic manuscripts), Manuscripta Islamica (Germany), Hill Museum & Manuscript Library
  • Citation network: Build the actual commentary graph — which texts cite which, across how many centuries
  • Student contributions: If you use this data in your essay, share your findings back. What did you discover that we missed?
