Checking the Claims

A response to "Why Did Islamic Science Decline?", checked against our dataset and the scholarly literature.

This video presents Eric Chaney's research clearly and accessibly. Several of its claims are well-supported. But some are overstated or missing context, and some deserve more careful treatment than a 20-minute video allows. We try to be fair both to the video and to our own limitations.

The Video's Source

The video draws primarily on Eric Chaney's "Religion and the Rise and Fall of Islamic Science" (Oxford, working paper). Chaney himself is more careful than the video suggests. He explicitly states that "the institutions empowering religious elites — not Islam itself — contributed to the decline" (p. 6) and concedes that time-varying selection in Western scholarship "could generate a spurious downward trend" (p. 16). The video drops both caveats.

The Claims

1. "Scientific output plummeted" (3:00)

Video says: Chaney found that "scientific output and also dissident thought plummeted" and that "during the Islamic Golden Age about 10% of works were scientific, but this dropped to 5% in 1100 and 2% in 1700."
Our data says: The proportional decline is real — Chaney's finding holds across multiple datasets. But total authors quintupled (4,333 in the 800s → 23,236 in the 1400s in our VIAF analysis). When adjusted for population (which fell from ~38 million to ~28 million due to Mongol invasions and the Black Death), scientists per million population rose from 0.82 to 1.44. The word "plummeted" describes proportional decline accurately but obscures absolute and per-capita growth.
Our limitation: Our VIAF dataset covers all pre-1500 authors globally, not specifically Islamicate authors. Our Islamicate subset (~9,500 by name pattern) is comparable in size to Chaney's VIAF component (~8,660) but was identified by Arabic name patterns, which may miss non-Arab Muslim scholars and include non-Muslim Arabic-named scholars. Population estimates carry 20-30% error margins.

Verdict: The proportional decline is real. But "plummeted" is misleading without normalising for the massive expansion of the educated population.

2. "The spread of madrasas caused the decline" (3:09)

Video says: The decline "went hand in hand with the spread of religious schools called madrasas" and "funding from rulers and wealthy elites shifted from secular libraries and scientific institutions to these religious institutions."
Our data says: El-Rouayheb (2008) documents that even Mehmed Birgevi (d. 1573), the inspiration for the puritanical Kadizadeli movement, classified mathematics and logic as fard kifaya (communal religious obligations). Ottoman colleges teaching the philosophical sciences may have doubled between 1650 and 1705. Of post-1100 integrated scholars in our dataset, biographical evidence consistently shows madrasa connections. The madrasa curriculum included logic, dialectic, and natural philosophy alongside fiqh and kalam.
Our limitation: We cannot prove that every polymath was madrasa-trained. Biographical evidence is strong for specific scholars (al-Tusi, Ibn al-Nafis, Ibn Khaldun, Ali Qushji) but is inference for others — madrasas were ubiquitous after 1100, so most scholars would have passed through one, but "most" is not "all." Chaney's correlation between madrasa spread and scientific proportion decline is statistically real. The question is interpretation.

Verdict: The correlation is real. But the interpretation (madrasas suppressed science) contradicts the evidence that madrasas taught rational sciences and trained the scholars who did science.

Sources: El-Rouayheb (2008, 2015); Brentjes (2018)

3. "Al-Ghazali was against philosophy / recommended death penalty" (1:25)

Video says: Al-Ghazali "recommended the death penalty for his philosophical opponents" and "it was the side of al-Ghazali that eventually won the day." But the video also quotes him saying (10:14): "A grievous crime against religion has been committed by a man who imagines that Islam is defended by the denial of the mathematical sciences."
Our data says: Al-Ghazali critiqued three specific metaphysical positions in the Tahafut (eternal universe, God not knowing particulars, no bodily resurrection). He did not condemn science, logic, or mathematics. Crucially: al-Ghazali's al-Mustasfa fi 'Ilm al-Usul (his foundational work on legal theory) opens with a substantial introduction to logic (mantiq), drawing directly on Ibn Sina's Kitab al-Shifa. He writes that mastery of logic is a prerequisite for anyone who claims to practice legal reasoning. By placing Avicennan logic at the foundation of usul al-fiqh, al-Ghazali ensured that logic would be taught in every madrasa where legal theory was studied — which was effectively all of them. The man accused of killing philosophy embedded philosophy's methods into the permanent curriculum of Islamic education.
Our limitation: We have not independently verified the content of al-Mustasfa. This claim relies on the scholarly consensus documented in El-Rouayheb (2008, 2015), Moosa (2005), and the broader secondary literature. Al-Ghazali's actual views are complex and debated. His call for takfir (excommunication) of the three positions is real and should not be minimised — it was a serious intervention with consequences.

Verdict: The video's own quotations contradict its framing. Al-Ghazali critiqued specific metaphysical claims while embedding logical methods into the legal curriculum. The picture is more complex than "he killed science."

Sources: al-Ghazali, al-Mustasfa fi 'Ilm al-Usul; Moosa (2005); El-Rouayheb (2008)

4. "An increase in commentaries suggests a drop in original thinking" (16:10)

Video says: Commentaries rose from under 10% to over 30% in the 15th century, suggesting decline in original thinking.
Our data says: Our VIAF title-based classification shows commentary at 6-14% across centuries — stable, not rising to 30%. The 30% figure comes from Hajji Khalifa's 17th-century Ottoman bibliographic encyclopedia, which was specifically a catalogue of scholarly works and their commentaries. Its design purpose over-represents commentaries relative to a general author census. Saliba (2007, p. 241) — whom Chaney himself cites — describes these commentaries as "in some ways superior to modern periodicals in their demand for comprehensive synthesis." Each commentary layer requires demonstrated mastery of all previous layers of engagement with the text.
Our limitation: Our VIAF-based commentary count relies on keyword detection ("sharh", "hashiya") in library catalogue titles, which may undercount commentaries whose titles don't include these terms. Chaney's Hajji Khalifa data may be more accurate for the Ottoman period specifically. The question of whether commentaries represent stagnation or depth is interpretive, not empirical — our data cannot settle it alone.

Verdict: The proportion may have risen in some datasets. But equating commentary with stagnation is an interpretation, not a finding. The scholarly counter-evidence (Saliba, El-Rouayheb) suggests these were sophisticated works of sustained engagement.

Sources: Saliba (2007), p. 241; El-Rouayheb (2015)

5. "Authors adhering to deviant interpretations declined from 4% to 0%" (16:37)

Video says: "It can't get more clear than that."
Our data says: This is Chaney's strongest finding and we do not dispute it. Authors identified with Mu'tazili or "rational Islam" positions did decline sharply in biographical dictionaries. What we add is context: El-Rouayheb (2008, 2015) demonstrates that the rational methods (logic, dialectic, philosophical theology) associated with these positions were absorbed into the Ash'ari mainstream and taught as standard madrasa subjects. The positions disappeared; the methods continued. This is analogous to a scientific paradigm being absorbed into the mainstream: the dissenting school vanishes because its useful ideas were incorporated.
Our limitation: We cannot determine whether the absorption of rational methods into orthodoxy preserved or diminished their vitality. A critic could argue that logic-as-tool-for-fiqh is less productive than logic-as-independent-inquiry. Our data on this is suggestive (logic and dialectic works continued to be produced in large numbers) but not conclusive.

Verdict: The decline in heterodox positions is real. The question is whether this represents intellectual narrowing or intellectual consolidation. Both readings are defensible. The video presents only one.

6. "Waqf funding couldn't be used against Islam" (17:21)

Video says: Waqf donations "by law could not be used against the tenants of Islam" and "religious leaders were presiding over these endowments," shifting money away from science.
Our data says: Waqf endowments funded hospitals (the Al-Mansuri Hospital in Cairo, 1284: 8,000 patient capacity, separate psychiatric wards, Quran readings and sound therapy, financial grants for discharged patients), observatories (al-Tusi's Maragheh observatory), and libraries across the Islamicate world. The waqf model funded SCHOLARS rather than PROJECTS, meaning a scholar could work across multiple fields without losing their endowment — the opposite of modern project-based grants that lock researchers to one narrow discipline.
Our limitation: We do not have systematic data on waqf allocation by discipline. The argument that waqf shifted resources toward religious education is plausible and documented by institutional historians. The question is whether this was a zero-sum shift (money to madrasas = money away from science) or an expansion (more total funding, with madrasas getting the new share). We don't have the data to settle this conclusively.

Verdict: The framing is one-sided. Waqf funded science, medicine, and education alongside religious learning. Whether the net effect was positive or negative for science is an open question.

7. "The decline spread east to west matching madrasa spread" (18:01)

Video says: The geographic correlation between madrasa spread and scientific proportion decline proves causation, and this rules out alternative explanations (Crusades, Mongols, Seljuks).
Our data says: Dallal (2010) argues the late decline was due to extrinsic factors: economic shifts following Europe's discovery of the New World, new military technologies, and colonialism. Keshavarzi (2025) documents that Ottomans were actively engaging with modern psychology on their own terms until the institutional break of 1923 (abolition of the caliphate, script change, closure of madrasas). The geographic spread of madrasas also correlates with the geographic spread of education in general.
Our limitation: Chaney's geographic analysis is methodologically careful and the correlation is statistically significant. Dallal's argument about extrinsic factors is qualitative, not quantitative. We cannot offer a competing quantitative geographic analysis. The correlation is real; the interpretation remains debated.

Verdict: The geographic correlation is real and significant. Whether it proves madrasas caused the decline or merely accompanied the expansion of education is an interpretive question the data alone cannot answer.

8. "Ibn Khaldun noted that Greek philosophy had almost disappeared" (12:29)

Video says: Ibn Khaldun (14th century) wrote that "the books and methods of the ancients are avoided as if they had never been" and that "theology is not something necessary to the contemporary student, for heretics and innovators have been destroyed."
Our data says: Ibn Khaldun was reporting on the decline of Greek philosophy as a standalone discipline. He was not reporting the end of rational inquiry. The video itself notes (13:01): "there were still those who studied logic, but now as an independent discipline, not as an instrument for the sciences." After Ibn Khaldun, Sacaklizade (d. 1732) believed there was MORE interest in philosophy in his time than in the 15th century (El-Rouayheb, 2008). Gelenbevi wrote a 609-page work on dialectic in the 18th century. The Ottoman tradition produced at least 88 works on psychology that remain largely untranslated (Keshavarzi, 2025).
Our limitation: Ibn Khaldun's testimony is a primary source and carries significant weight. He was there; we were not. His observation that Greek philosophy declined as a discipline is probably accurate. The question is whether this represents the death of rational inquiry or its transformation into other forms. El-Rouayheb's counter-evidence is strong but focuses on the Ottoman period (post-Ibn Khaldun). The gap between Ibn Khaldun (d. 1406) and El-Rouayheb's period of focus (17th century) is two centuries we can't fully account for.

Verdict: Ibn Khaldun accurately reported the decline of Greek philosophy as a standalone discipline. But equating this with the death of rational inquiry ignores the evidence of continued logical, dialectical, and philosophical work in subsequent centuries.

What the Video Doesn't Mention

These aren't criticisms of the video — a 20-minute format requires compression. But they are things a viewer would need to know for a complete picture:

Missing ContextWhy It Matters
Population normalisationThe Islamicate world's population dropped ~26% (Mongols, Black Death, Timur). Per-capita output rose. No decline narrative can ignore demographics.
Absolute vs proportionalEvery scientific field grew in absolute terms in our dataset. Medicine 6.3x, philosophy 6.5x, mathematics 4.2x. "Decline" is proportional only.
Al-Ghazali's al-MustasfaAl-Ghazali opened his foundational work on legal theory with a section on Avicennan logic from Ibn Sina's Kitab al-Shifa, making logic a prerequisite for legal reasoning and ensuring it was taught in every madrasa. The man accused of killing philosophy embedded its methods in the permanent curriculum.
Women scholarsAl-Sakhawi documented 1,530 women scholars in the 15th century alone (268 received ijaza, 142 studied hadith). VIAF has 3 from the same period. An entire dimension of intellectual life is invisible to the framework.
Undigitised manuscriptsAn estimated 3-7 million Islamic manuscripts survive. ~1.5 million are in any catalogue. All decline claims are based on incomplete evidence (Brentjes, 2018).
ColonialismThe video does not mention colonialism once. The 1923 Turkish script change made 170,000+ Ottoman manuscripts functionally inaccessible overnight. The real institutional break was colonial, not Ghazalian.

Our Own Limitations

We want to be honest about what our analysis can and cannot do:

  • Our VIAF dataset covers all pre-1500 authors globally, not specifically Islamicate authors. Filtering by name pattern introduces both false positives and false negatives.
  • Our AI classification (Claude Sonnet 4.6) depends on transliterated library titles and AI training data. It is not a substitute for domain-expert classification.
  • Our commentary count (6-14%) may be an undercount if many commentaries aren't labelled as such in library catalogues.
  • Chaney's finding of proportional decline is statistically robust across multiple datasets. We do not dispute the finding. We dispute the interpretation.
  • The question of whether the Islamic world experienced genuine intellectual decline relative to its own earlier output (as opposed to relative to Europe's acceleration) remains genuinely open.
  • We have a perspective. This atlas was built by a student of Islamic psychology who believes the tradition has resources the modern world needs. That perspective shapes what we emphasise. We have tried to be fair, but we are not neutral.
