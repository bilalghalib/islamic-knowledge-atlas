Why This Exists

Numbers always live inside interpretive frames. Here is the frame, and what happens when you change it.

The Question

Did Islamic science decline? A growing body of quantitative scholarship says yes. Eric Chaney's "Religion and the Rise and Fall of Islamic Science" (2016/2023) is the most methodologically careful version of this argument. He counts scholars in biographical dictionaries, finds the proportion classified as "scientists" dropped from ~4% to ~1.4% between the 800s and 1400s, and attributes the decline to the political empowerment of religious leaders.

Chaney is careful. He blames "institutions empowering religious elites, not Islam itself" (p. 6). He concedes that Western scholarship may have "systematically ignored the works of later scientists," producing a "spurious downward trend" (p. 16). His data is real.

But data doesn't interpret itself. The same numbers, received by a different interpretive frame, tell a different story.

The Interpretive Frame

Chaney's framework makes three assumptions:

1. Science and religion are separate categories

Each scholar is classified as either a scientist or not. But the tradition didn't recognise this boundary. Mehmed Birgevi (d. 1573), the inspiration for the puritanical Kadizadeli movement, classified mathematics and logic as fard kifaya (communal religious obligations). A mathematician in 17th-century Istanbul was fulfilling a religious duty. Chaney's framework sees a scientist. The tradition saw a scholar discharging communal obligation. Both are true. Neither is the whole truth.

2. Proportion is the right measure of health

If you have 100 scholars and 4 are scientists, that's 4%. If you then educate 1,000 more people and 14 of them are scientists, that's 1.4%. Science "declined" by 63%. But you have 3.5x more scientists than before, serving a broader educated population. The "decline" is democratisation measured with an aristocratic ruler.

3. The commentary tradition is stagnation

Chaney links rising commentaries to "channeling intellectual production towards reproducing approved knowledge." But Saliba (2007) — whom Chaney cites — calls these commentaries "in some ways superior to modern periodicals in their demand for comprehensive synthesis." Each commentary layer requires mastery of all previous layers. That's more intellectually demanding than writing an "original" work from scratch.

What We Did

We processed the complete VIAF (Virtual International Authority File) 2024 dump: 85,043 pre-1500 author records with work titles. We classified 35,606 scholars using Claude Sonnet 4.6, with two layers:

  • Title evidence: what the library catalog titles actually say (conservative, fully defensible)
  • Background knowledge: what the AI knows about the scholar beyond the titles (broader, with Wikipedia references)

Each classification has a confidence score (0-100), so you can filter by how certain the evidence is. Every claim is traceable to a specific title or a specific Wikipedia article.

What We Found

Our title-based classification finds 6.5% scientists across all centuries — nearly 5x Chaney's 1.4% for the 1400s. The gap is largest in exactly the centuries he identifies as the steepest decline. Every field grows in absolute terms: medicine 6.3x, philosophy 6.5x, mathematics 4.2x. No domain declines.

The difference isn't about data quality. Our VIAF extract (~9,500 MENA authors by name pattern) is comparable in size to Chaney's VIAF component (~8,660). The difference is classification method: he uses occupation labels from biographical dictionaries (one box per scholar), we classify by work titles (catches polymaths who wrote across domains).

Psychology and the Soul

This project emerged from an essay for the Cambridge Muslim College PGCert in Islam & Psychology. The connection is not accidental.

The word "psychology" comes from psyche (soul) + logos (study). Psychology was the study of the soul. Over the past century, it moved toward studying neural activity, cognitive processes, and behaviour — measurable things. The soul, being unmeasurable, was quietly dropped.

The same move that makes tasawwuf (the science of spiritual states) "not science" in Chaney's framework is the move that makes the ruh (spirit) "not psychology" in modern clinical practice. Both are acts of category narrowing: the container decides what counts before the content arrives.

As Abdal Hakim Murad observes: "Once we said know thyself. Nowadays we say show thyself." The shift from knowledge as reception (the porous self, open to what flows from beyond) to knowledge as production (the buffered self, sealed within its own certainty) is the same shift that makes a 700-year commentary tradition look like stagnation to one frame and like living knowledge architecture to another.

What's Missing from the Data

An estimated 3-7 million Islamic manuscripts survive worldwide. About 1.5 million are in any published catalogue. Our VIAF data covers 85,043 records. We are working with roughly 1-3% of what exists.

Iran — 400,000+ catalogued, 140,000 uncatalogued

Astan Quds Razavi (Mashhad): ~72,000 volumes. Ayatollah Mar'ashi Najafi (Qom): 250,000+ volumes. Malek National Library (Tehran): ~19,000. Plus 140,000 uncatalogued manuscripts in private and seminary collections. Languages: Arabic, Persian.

Timbuktu — 350,000-400,000

Across 35+ libraries, including 31 private family collections. Ahmed Baba Institute: ~10,500 on-site. Subjects: astronomy, medicine, mathematics, law, poetry. 150,000 photographed by HMML but fewer than 1,400 accessible online. 300,000 were smuggled to Bamako during the 2012 crisis to save them.

Turkey — 170,000+ (all scanned, almost none translated)

Suleymaniye Library (Istanbul): ~100,000 manuscripts (60% Arabic, 30% Ottoman Turkish, 10% Persian). All scanned between 2002-2011. But the 1928 script reform means most Turkish citizens cannot read Ottoman Turkish. 88+ psychology works identified by Keshavarzi remain untranslated. The manuscripts are digitised but functionally inaccessible.

Egypt — 107,000 manuscripts

Dar al-Kutub (Cairo): ~57,000 manuscripts (88,000 titles). Al-Azhar Library: ~50,000 items comprising 7 million pages, only ~33% digitised. No comprehensive open-access digital repository exists for either collection.

Morocco — 84,000+

National Library (Rabat): ~80,000 manuscripts (200,000 titles). Al-Qarawiyyin Library (Fez): 4,000+ manuscripts, ~20% digitised. Royal Library (Hassaniyya): unknown size, access restricted to the royal palace. The oldest continuously operating library in the world (founded 859 CE) remains mostly undigitised.

India — 38,000+ documented

Khuda Bakhsh (Patna): 21,000+ manuscripts in Arabic, Persian, Urdu, Turkish, Sanskrit. Rampur Raza Library: 17,000 manuscripts, ~59% digitised. Plus uncatalogued collections at Nadwat al-Ulama (Lucknow), Salar Jung Museum (Hyderabad), and mosque libraries across the subcontinent.

Brentjes (2018) puts it directly: "We have yet to identify the vast majority of teaching texts on the physical, mathematical and metaphysical sciences." All decline claims are premature because we haven't counted what's there.

"The image of decline in the twelfth to fifteenth centuries is not the product of research in manuscript archives, but an assumption made in the absence of research and encouraged for its usefulness as a tool in religious polemics." — Lindberg (2007), via Aftab

A Note on Tone

This project is not an attack on Chaney or on quantitative approaches to intellectual history. Chaney's paper is methodologically careful and raises genuine questions about the political economy of knowledge production. The critique here is of the interpretive frame, not the data.

Numbers are real. Interpretation is a choice. The same data that shows "declining proportion of scientists" also shows "expanding educated population producing more scholars per capita during civilisational catastrophe." Both readings are true. The question is which frame serves understanding and which serves a narrative.

This atlas is offered as a tool for asking better questions, not as a weapon for winning arguments.
