# UNDIGITIZED ISLAMIC MANUSCRIPT COLLECTIONS WORLDWIDE

## Global Overview

- **Al-Furqan Islamic Heritage Foundation** estimates **3 million** Islamic manuscripts have survived
- Other scholars estimate **7 million** surviving manuscripts out of an estimated **90 million** written between the 7th and 14th centuries
- The **World Survey of Islamic Manuscripts** documented ~1.5 million manuscripts across 2,500+ collections
- Sabine Schmidtke (Institute for Advanced Study) writes that "what is available online, whether published or in manuscript form, is only the tip of the iceberg" -- much material remains "unaccounted for"
- The **total number of all extant manuscripts in Arabic script is not known**

---

## 1. IRAN

### Overall Scale
- The **Union Catalogue of Manuscripts in Iranian Libraries** (2011, 35 volumes) lists approximately **400,000 manuscripts** in Arabic and Persian -- **excluding** private collections
- Approximately **140,000 manuscripts remain uncatalogued** in Iran; manuscripts in private collections constitute roughly half of those uncatalogued
- Of the uncatalogued manuscripts, ~7,000 are in the National Library alone, with similar or larger numbers in other major libraries

### Major Collections

**Astan Quds Razavi Central Library, Mashhad**
- ~30,250 manuscript volumes + 25,000 lithographic books + 17,240 other handwritten materials (~72,490 volumes as of 2003 count)
- Part of a broader collection exceeding 1.1 million volumes
- Languages: Arabic, Persian
- Subjects: Quranic manuscripts, Islamic sciences, rare historical works -- described as "the largest collection of its kind in the world"
- Digitization: ~3 million digital information sources created (manuscripts, lithographs, maps, treatises); substantial digitization completed

**Malek National Library and Museum, Tehran**
- ~19,000 manuscripts
- Languages: Arabic, Persian, English, French, others
- Digital library operational since October 2007
- Cataloguing completed (9 volumes)
- One of "six great manuscript treasures" in Iran

**Majlis (Parliament) Library, Tehran**
- Major collection (specific total not confirmed in searches, but one of Iran's largest)
- Manuscripts digitized and searchable through online database
- Languages: Arabic, Persian

**Ayatollah Mar'ashi Najafi Library, Qom**
- 250,000+ volumes (largest Islamic manuscript library in Iran, third largest in the Islamic world)
- ~25,000 available online
- Core seminary library; Islamic jurisprudence, theology, philosophy

**Other Qom Seminary Libraries**
- Hujjatiyya Seminary: ~1,040 manuscripts + 3,500 lithographic books
- Multiple additional seminary libraries with uncatalogued holdings
- Many private scholarly collections remain entirely uncatalogued

### Digitization Status
- Major libraries (Majlis, Tehran University, Malek, Islamic Heritage Revival Centre in Qom) are **digitized**
- However, the 140,000 uncatalogued manuscripts (many in private/seminary collections) remain largely **undigitized**
- Digital access is often limited to in-library terminals rather than open web access

## 2. TIMBUKTU / MALI

### Scale
- **350,000 to 400,000 manuscripts** documented across 35-45 libraries
- 300,000+ were smuggled to Bamako during the 2012 crisis; ~28,000 repatriated to Timbuktu as of August 2025
- The **Ahmed Baba Institute (IHERI-AB)** holds ~10,487 manuscripts on-site

### Languages & Subjects
- Primarily **Arabic**, with some in local African languages (Songhai, Tamashek)
- Subjects: Islamic religious texts, **astronomy**, **medicine**, **mathematics**, diplomatic correspondence, legal documents, poetry, history, trade records

### Family Libraries
- 31+ private family libraries documented (Cambridge study)
- Traditional family stewardship: members swear publicly to protect their library for life
- **SAVAMA-DCI** (the NGO) organized the rescue of 90% of manuscripts in 2012

### Digitization Status
- Before 2012 crisis: only **~20%** of Ahmed Baba Institute manuscripts were digitized
- Now: nearly all Ahmed Baba manuscripts are digitized, with backups on external servers
- **HMML (Hill Museum & Manuscript Library)** has photographed **150,000+ manuscripts** in partnership with SAVAMA-DCI
- But only **~1,400 digital copies** are publicly accessible via HMML Virtual Reading Room
- Most family library manuscripts remain **undigitized or inaccessible online**

## 3. TURKEY

### Scale
- **Suleymaniye Library, Istanbul**: ~100,000 manuscript volumes + 50,000 printed books -- one of the world's largest Islamic manuscript collections
- **Total in Turkish government libraries**: 28,106 Turkish + 129,121 Arabic + 8,776 Persian manuscripts (under General Directorate of Libraries alone)
- **Istanbul University Library**: 9,941 Turkish + 6,963 Arabic + 1,615 Persian manuscripts
- **Topkapi Palace Museum Library**: 3,088 Turkish + 9,043 Arabic + 939 Persian manuscripts

### Language Composition (Suleymaniye)
- ~60% Arabic
- ~30% Turkish (Ottoman Turkish)
- ~10% Persian
- Plus scattered manuscripts in other languages

### Subjects
- Law and jurisprudence, belles-lettres, morality/sermons, logic, rhetoric, grammar, sciences, medicine, astronomy, history

### Digitization Status
- Suleymaniye: **all manuscripts digitized** (2002-2011 campaign), accessible on in-library computers; ~10,000 printed works still to be digitized
- However, "digitized" means photographed/scanned -- **the vast majority remain untranslated** from Ottoman Turkish (Arabic script) into modern Turkish or any European language
- The 1928 script reform created a linguistic barrier: most Turkish citizens today cannot read Ottoman Turkish, meaning these manuscripts are functionally inaccessible even when digitized

### What's "Missing"
- The sheer volume (~170,000+ manuscripts in government libraries alone) combined with the Ottoman script barrier means most of this material has never been studied by Western or even modern Turkish scholars
- No reliable estimate exists for what percentage has been critically edited or translated

## 4. INDIA

### Khuda Bakhsh Oriental Public Library, Patna
- **21,000+ manuscripts**
- Languages: Arabic, Persian, Urdu, Turkish, Pashto, Sanskrit, Hindi
- Includes rare and unique manuscripts
- Established 1891, autonomous body under Ministry of Culture
- Online manuscript catalogues available
- Digitization status: catalogues published but extent of full digitization unclear from available sources

### Rampur Raza Library, Uttar Pradesh
- **17,000 manuscripts** + ~30,000 printed books
- Languages: Arabic, Persian, Urdu, Sanskrit, Hindi, Turkish
- Established 1774 by Nawab Faizullah Khan
- Subjects: Indo-Islamic learning, arts, religious works, calligraphy, miniature paintings, astronomical instruments
- Digitization: **10,000 of 17,000 manuscripts digitized** (~59%); ~8 million pages (25,000 items) digitized overall

### Osmania University Library, Hyderabad
- Significant Islamic manuscript collection (specific count not confirmed in search results)
- Part of the broader Hyderabad cluster which also includes:
  - **Salar Jung Museum Library** -- major collection
  - **Andhra Pradesh State Government Oriental Manuscripts Library**
- Hyderabad was historically one of the richest centers of Islamic learning in India

### Other Notable Indian Collections
- **Nadwat al-Ulama Library, Lucknow** -- significant Arabic/Persian holdings
- Numerous mosque and madrasa libraries across India with uncatalogued holdings

## 5. MOROCCO

### Al-Qarawiyyin Library, Fez
- **4,000+ manuscripts**
- Includes a 9th-century Quran in Kufic script on camel skin
- Founded 859 CE -- oldest continuously operating library in the world
- Subjects: Islamic sciences, jurisprudence, astronomy, medicine, grammar, Sufism
- Languages: Arabic, some Amazigh
- Digitization: **~20% digitized** as of available reports; new digitization lab established during 2016-2017 restoration (led by architect Aziza Chaouni); partnership with Institute of Computational Linguistics (Italy)

### National Library of the Kingdom of Morocco (Bibliotheque Nationale), Rabat
- **~80,000 manuscripts** representing ~200,000 titles
- Also holds ~60,000 rare books
- Languages: Arabic, French, other
- Part of the broader collection of 340,000+ works

### Royal Library (Khizana al-Hassaniyya), Rabat
- Housed within the royal palace, directly attached to royal residences
- One of Morocco's major manuscript collections, property of King Mohammed VI
- **Specific collection size not publicly documented** -- access is restricted
- Contains rare and unique manuscripts not found elsewhere

## 6. EGYPT

### Dar al-Kutub (Egyptian National Library), Cairo
- **~57,000 manuscripts** (51,193 conservation numbers = 59,321 volumes = 88,164 titles)
- Formed in 1870 by gathering manuscripts from mosques, government offices, and endowment treasuries
- Languages: Arabic, Persian, Turkish
- Subjects: Quran, Islamic jurisprudence, philosophy, sciences, literature, history
- Digitization: Much (not all) of the collection filmed on microform; digital copies available on CD by request; **no comprehensive open-access digital repository**
- UNESCO has recognized the Dar al-Kutub manuscripts collection

### Al-Azhar Library, Cairo
- **~50,000 separate manuscript items**, comprising approximately **7 million pages**
- Languages: Primarily Arabic; some Persian, Turkish
- Subjects: Islamic theology, jurisprudence (fiqh), Hadith, Quranic sciences, philosophy, logic, medicine, astronomy
- Digitization: **approximately one-third (~33%) digitized** in JPG format, viewable folio by folio on library machines
- Online digitization project underway ("al-Azhar online project")

### Other Cairo institutions
- **Central Library of Islamic Manuscripts (Awqaf Library)** -- significant holdings, separate from Dar al-Kutub
- **Bibliotheca Alexandrina** -- also engaged in manuscript digitization
- **Ma'had al-Makhtutat (Institute of Arabic Manuscripts)** -- cataloguing body

## SCHOLARLY ESTIMATES OF WHAT'S "MISSING"

### Sonja Brentjes (2018)
- In *Teaching and Learning the Sciences in Islamicate Societies (800-1700)* (Brepols), Brentjes argues that the identification of teaching texts remains deeply incomplete. The book highlights that it is "often unclear whether a specific text was indeed read between a teacher and one or more students," making it very difficult to determine what was actually taught. The broader implication is that **we have yet to identify the vast majority of teaching texts** used in Islamic educational institutions -- the madrasa curriculum is reconstructed from a tiny fraction of what circulated.
- Source: [Isis review, Vol 110, No 4](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/706884); [Brepols listing](https://www.brepols.net/products/IS-9782503574455-1)

### Sabine Schmidtke (Institute for Advanced Study)
- In "The Written Heritage of the Muslim World" (Getty Publications, *Cultural Heritage and Mass Atrocities*): "what is available online, whether published or in manuscript form, is **only the tip of the iceberg**." Much of the written cultural production of the Islamic world is preserved only in manuscript form, and despite generations of scholars' work, "**the riches of most collections remain largely unexplored**."
- Source: [Getty Publications](https://www.getty.edu/publications/cultural-heritage-mass-atrocities/part-1/05-schmidtke/)

### UCLA Islamicate Manuscripts Initiative
- States that even major Western collections "remain unexplored, under-described and inaccessible."
- Source: [UCLA Library](https://www.library.ucla.edu/about/programs/islamicate-initiative/)

### The Scale of the Unknown
- If 3-7 million manuscripts survive, and the World Survey documented ~1.5 million, that means **50-80% of surviving manuscripts may not even be in a published catalogue**
- Private collections in Iran, Yemen, West Africa, South/Southeast Asia, and Central Asia remain the largest gap
- The ratio of manuscripts that have received **critical scholarly editions** is far smaller still -- likely in the low single-digit percentages of the total corpus
