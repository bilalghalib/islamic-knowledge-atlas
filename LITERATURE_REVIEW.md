# Academic Literature on "Decline of Islamic Science" Narratives

## 1. Quantitative/Data-Driven "Decline" Arguments

### Eric Chaney (2016, not 2023)
- **Title:** "Religion and the Rise and Fall of Islamic Science"
- **Published:** Working paper, Harvard University (versions circulated from ~2016)
- **Main argument:** The increasing political power of religious leaders after the fall of the Abbasid caliphate led to a decline in scientific output. Uses a dataset of notable scholars from the Islamic world.
- **Data source:** Biographical dictionaries; counts of "scientists" vs "religious scholars" over centuries using entries in the *Encyclopedia of Islam* and similar reference works.
- **Key finding:** Ratio of religious to scientific scholars increased after ~1100 CE; regions with stronger religious establishment saw less scientific output. Argues political economy of religious authority crowded out science.
- **Note:** This is the paper you referenced. It has circulated as a working paper and been presented widely. The quantitative approach (counting scholars in encyclopedias) has drawn methodological criticism.

### Benoit de Courson & Nicolas Baumard (2022–2023)
- **Title:** "Quantifying the rise and fall of scientific activity in pre-modern cities" (and related papers)
- **Published:** Various versions; associated with the Cultural Evolution / cliometric literature
- **Main argument:** Uses city-level data on book production and notable individuals to map intellectual activity over time, including in the Islamic world.
- **Data source:** Databases of historical notable persons, book production estimates.
- **Key finding:** Shows a peak and subsequent decline in Islamic intellectual output, though frames it as part of broader global patterns rather than religion-specific.

### Bas van Bavel, Maaike van Berkel, and collaborators
- **Title:** Various papers on economic and institutional history of the medieval Islamic world
- **Main argument:** Institutional "ossification" including waqf (endowment) systems contributed to stagnation.
- **Data source:** Economic and institutional records, waqf documents.
- **Key finding:** Property and endowment regimes became rigid, limiting adaptive capacity of educational and scientific institutions.

### Jared Rubin (2017)
- **Title:** *Rulers, Religion, and Riches: Why the West Got Rich and the Middle East Did Not*
- **Published:** Cambridge University Press, 2017
- **Main argument:** Muslim rulers relied on religious legitimacy from clerics, giving them veto power over institutions. European rulers shifted to legitimation through merchants/economic elites, enabling institutional innovation.
- **Data source:** Comparative institutional analysis; printing press adoption data (the Ottoman ban on Arabic-script printing until 1729 is a central case).
- **Key finding:** The "bargain" between rulers and religious authorities in the Islamic world was the key mechanism, not Islam per se but the political economy of religious authority.

### Timur Kuran (2011)
- **Title:** *The Long Divergence: How Islamic Law Held Back the Middle East*
- **Published:** Princeton University Press, 2011
- **Main argument:** Specific features of Islamic commercial and inheritance law (partnership rules, inheritance fragmentation) prevented the development of complex organizational forms (corporations, banks).
- **Data source:** Ottoman court records, waqf documents, commercial contracts across several centuries.
- **Key finding:** Islamic legal institutions that were initially adaptive became obstacles when economic complexity increased. Emphasizes law and institutions rather than theology, but the framing ("Islamic law held back") places it in the decline literature.

### Toby Huff (2003, 2nd ed. 2017)
- **Title:** *The Rise of Early Modern Science: Islam, China, and the West*
- **Published:** Cambridge University Press
- **Main argument:** Institutional structures in the Islamic world (and China) lacked the autonomous corporate bodies (universities, professional guilds) that enabled the Scientific Revolution in Europe.
- **Data source:** Comparative institutional history; madrasa curricula vs. European university curricula.
- **Key finding:** Madrasas were legally restricted to religious sciences; natural philosophy never gained institutional autonomy. The European university's corporate legal status was decisive.

## 2. Religion vs. Reason / Revelation vs. Science Framing

### Dominique Urvoy (1996)
- **Title:** Various works on Ibn Rushd and the "defeat of rationalism" in Islam
- **Main argument:** The Ash'arite theological victory over Mu'tazilite rationalism, and al-Ghazali's critique of philosophy, ended the productive engagement of Islamic thought with Greek rationalism.

### Pervez Hoodbhoy (1991)
- **Title:** *Islam and Science: Religious Orthodoxy and the Battle for Rationality*
- **Published:** Zed Books, 1991
- **Main argument:** Orthodox Islam is fundamentally inhospitable to scientific inquiry due to occasionalist theology (God directly causes all events, undermining natural causation).
- **Data source:** Theological texts, historical narrative, and contemporary survey of science in Muslim-majority countries.
- **Key finding:** Ash'arite occasionalism destroyed the concept of natural law necessary for science. Al-Ghazali's *Tahafut al-Falasifa* is treated as the turning point.

### Taner Edis (2007)
- **Title:** *An Illusion of Harmony: Science and Religion in Islam*
- **Published:** Prometheus Books, 2007
- **Main argument:** Contemporary claims of harmony between Islam and science are superficial; deep theological commitments in mainstream Islam are in tension with naturalistic science.
- **Data source:** Analysis of contemporary Islamic discourse on science, plus historical material.
- **Key finding:** Neither the "Golden Age" narrative nor the "harmony" narrative withstands scrutiny; the relationship is more conflicted than either side admits.

### Hillel Ofek (2011)
- **Title:** "Why the Arabic World Turned Away from Science"
- **Published:** *The New Atlantis*, No. 30, Winter 2011
- **Main argument:** A popular but widely cited essay arguing al-Ghazali's theological occasionalism killed Islamic science.
- **Key finding:** Presents the standard "Ghazali killed science" narrative with accessible framing. Not peer-reviewed scholarship but influential in policy/public discourse.

## 3. The Classic "Conflict Thesis" and Its Modern Inheritors

### John William Draper (1874)
- **Title:** *History of the Conflict between Religion and Science*
- **Published:** D. Appleton & Co., 1874
- **Main argument:** Religion (primarily Catholicism, but extended to Islam in later reception) is inherently at war with science. Islam is treated ambiguously: praised for preserving Greek learning but criticized as ultimately dogmatic.
- **Key finding:** Established the "conflict thesis" framework. Islam appears as a temporary custodian of Greek knowledge before Europe reclaimed it.

### Andrew Dickson White (1896)
- **Title:** *A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom*
- **Published:** D. Appleton & Co., 1896, 2 volumes
- **Main argument:** Theology (distinct from "true religion") wages war on science. Primarily about Christianity but the framework was applied to Islam by later scholars.
- **Key finding:** Codified the "warfare" metaphor. Less directly about Islam than Draper, but the framework became the template for "religion kills science" arguments applied cross-culturally.

### Modern inheritors of the conflict thesis in Islamic context:
- **Steven Weinberg** — Nobel physicist who has made public statements (lectures, essays) arguing Islam became hostile to science after al-Ghazali. See his 2007 essay "Without God" and various public lectures.
- **Neil deGrasse Tyson** — His widely viewed remarks on al-Ghazali "destroying" Islamic science (Cosmos, public lectures) popularized this narrative. Later partially walked back.
- **Sam Harris** — *The End of Faith* (2004) and subsequent works frame Islam as uniquely hostile to rational inquiry.

### Important historiographic note:
The conflict thesis has been **thoroughly debunked** for Christianity by historians of science (John Hedley Brooke, Ronald Numbers, David Lindberg). The application to Islam often lags behind this historiographic correction.

## 4. Quantitative Studies Framing Data as Decline

### Sonja Brentjes & Robert Morrison (2010)
- **Title:** "The Sciences in Islamic Societies"
- **Published:** *The New Cambridge History of Islam*, Vol. 4
- **Note:** While Brentjes is a *critic* of decline narratives (see Section 6), this chapter surveys the data landscape and engages with the quantitative claims.

### Morgan Clarke & various collaborators — Prosopographic databases
- Multiple projects have built databases of Islamic scholars from biographical dictionaries (*tabaqat* literature). These databases underlie many quantitative claims.
- **Key methodological issue:** What counts as "science"? Biographical dictionaries are biased toward religious scholars by design. Counting entries produces artifacts that look like "decline of science" but may reflect "growth of biographical interest in religious scholars."

### Maxim Romanov (2017+)
- **Title:** Various digital humanities projects on Arabic biographical dictionaries
- **Published:** Multiple papers and the OpenITI project
- **Main contribution:** Built computational tools for analyzing Arabic biographical collections at scale. His work reveals how much the "decline" picture depends on which sources you count and how you categorize scholars.
- **Key finding:** The data is far more complex than simple decline narratives suggest; many "religious" scholars also did mathematical, astronomical, or medical work.

## 5. Counter-Arguments: Scholars Who Challenge Decline Narratives

### George Saliba (2007)
- **Title:** *Islamic Science and the Making of the European Renaissance*
- **Published:** MIT Press, 2007
- **Main argument:** Islamic astronomy continued to produce innovative, mathematically sophisticated work well into the 15th-16th centuries. The "decline after 1100" narrative is empirically false.
- **Data source:** Astronomical texts, particularly the Maragha school (13th-14th c.) and their influence on Copernicus.
- **Key finding:** Ibn al-Shatir (14th c. Damascus) produced planetary models mathematically identical to Copernicus's later work. Science didn't decline; it continued and likely *influenced* the European Scientific Revolution. Saliba argues the standard narrative is an artifact of European-language historiography ignoring Arabic manuscripts.

### F. Jamil Ragep (2019 and various)
- **Title:** "Islamic Culture and the Natural Sciences" and multiple papers
- **Published:** Various, including in *The Cambridge History of Science*
- **Main argument:** The sharp religion/science divide is anachronistic when applied to the Islamic world. Many madrasa-affiliated scholars did cutting-edge astronomy and mathematics.
- **Data source:** Madrasa curricula, astronomical texts produced within madrasa contexts, biographical evidence.
- **Key finding:** The claim that madrasas excluded the sciences is factually wrong for many times and places. Scholars like Ali Qushji (15th c. Samarkand) did innovative astronomy *within* religious institutional settings.

### Khaled El-Rouayheb (2015)
- **Title:** *Islamic Intellectual History in the Seventeenth Century: Scholarly Currents in the Ottoman Empire and the Maghreb*
- **Published:** Cambridge University Press, 2015
- **Main argument:** The Ottoman 17th century — supposedly the nadir of Islamic intellectual life — was actually a period of vigorous scholarly debate, including in logic, philosophy, and theology.
- **Data source:** Manuscript evidence from Ottoman and Maghrebi libraries; detailed analysis of scholarly debates.
- **Key finding:** The "decline" narrative is an artifact of not reading the sources. Hundreds of sophisticated works in logic, dialectics, and philosophy were produced in the supposedly "dead" period. El-Rouayheb's work is devastating to simple decline narratives because he actually reads the manuscripts.

### Khaled El-Rouayheb & Sabine Schmidtke, eds. (2016)
- **Title:** *The Oxford Handbook of Islamic Philosophy*
- **Published:** Oxford University Press, 2016
- **Main argument:** Islamic philosophy did not end with Ibn Rushd (12th c.) as the standard narrative claims. Rich philosophical traditions continued in the Eastern Islamic world through the early modern period.

### Sonja Brentjes (various, 2000s-2020s)
- **Title:** Multiple articles including "The Prison of Categories: 'Decline' and Its Company" and works on mathematical sciences in Islamic societies
- **Main argument:** The categories used to measure "decline" (science vs. religion, rational vs. traditional) are imported from European intellectual history and distort the Islamic material.
- **Data source:** Mathematical and astronomical manuscripts, cross-referencing with institutional and biographical data.
- **Key finding:** When you look at actual manuscript production, teaching, and practice rather than applying European periodization, the picture is one of transformation and regional variation, not uniform decline.

### Ahmad Dallal (2010)
- **Title:** *Islam, Science, and the Challenge of History*
- **Published:** Yale University Press, 2010
- **Main argument:** Islamic scientific traditions were deeply embedded in Islamic intellectual culture and not in conflict with religious commitments. The "decline" framing misunderstands how science functioned in Islamic societies.
- **Data source:** Scientific texts from across the Islamic world, 9th-18th centuries.
- **Key finding:** Scientific activity continued robustly but in different institutional forms than European science. The "decline" is partly real (relative to Europe's acceleration) but is massively overstated and misdiagnosed.

### Ahmad Dallal (2018)
- **Title:** *Islam Without Europe: Traditions of Reform in Eighteenth-Century Islamic Thought*
- **Published:** University of North Carolina Press, 2018
- **Main argument:** 18th-century Islamic intellectual life was dynamic and self-reforming, not stagnant. The narrative of decline followed by European-induced modernity is false.

### Dimitri Gutas (1998)
- **Title:** *Greek Thought, Arabic Culture: The Graeco-Arabic Translation Movement in Baghdad and Early Abbasid Society*
- **Published:** Routledge, 1998
- **Main argument:** The translation movement was driven by political and social factors, not "Islam discovering Greek rationalism." This reframing matters because it challenges the idea that Islamic science was derivative.
- **Key finding:** The translation movement was a deliberate political project of the Abbasid caliphate, motivated by imperial ideology. This complicates both "Islam was hospitable to science" and "Islam killed science" narratives.

## 6. Decline Narratives as Colonial/Postcolonial Scholarship

### Abdulhamid Sabra (1987)
- **Title:** "The Appropriation and Subsequent Naturalization of Greek Science in Medieval Islam: A Preliminary Statement"
- **Published:** *History of Science*, 25(3), 1987
- **Main argument:** Introduced the concept of "naturalization" — Greek science was not passively received but actively transformed by Islamic scholars. Challenges the "preservation" narrative that reduces Islamic science to a holding operation for Europe.

### Marwa Elshakry (2013)
- **Title:** *Reading Darwin in Arabic, 1860-1950*
- **Published:** University of Chicago Press, 2013
- **Main argument:** The reception of evolutionary theory in the Arabic world was sophisticated and varied, not simply "rejection by religion." Colonial power dynamics shaped how these debates were framed.
- **Key finding:** The "Islam vs. science" framing was partly a product of colonial encounters and missionary polemics, not an accurate description of internal Islamic debates.

### Ebrahim Moosa (2005)
- **Title:** *Ghazali and the Poetics of Imagination*
- **Published:** University of North Carolina Press, 2005
- **Main argument:** Al-Ghazali has been caricatured by both Western scholars and Muslim modernists. His actual engagement with philosophy and science was far more nuanced than "he killed philosophy."
- **Key finding:** The "Ghazali killed Islamic science" narrative is a modern construction that misreads his texts. Ghazali critiqued specific metaphysical claims of the philosophers while endorsing mathematics, logic, and empirical inquiry.

### Syed Nomanul Haq (various)
- Has written extensively on how colonial historiography constructed the "decline" narrative to justify European rule over Muslim societies. The argument: "Your civilization declined because of your religion; we are here to modernize you."

### Wael Hallaq (2018)
- **Title:** *Restating Orientalism: A Critique of Modern Knowledge*
- **Published:** Columbia University Press, 2018
- **Main argument:** The entire framework of "Islamic decline" is embedded in Orientalist knowledge structures that position Europe as the normative trajectory of civilization.

### Rushain Abbasi (2020)
- **Title:** "Islam and the Invention of Religion: A Study of Medieval Muslim Discourses on Din"
- **Published:** *Studia Islamica*, 2020 (and subsequent book project)
- **Main argument:** The category "religion" as a bounded domain separate from "science" or "politics" is a modern Western construction. Applying it retroactively to Islamic history creates false oppositions.

### Cemil Aydin (2017)
- **Title:** *The Idea of the Muslim World: A Global Intellectual History*
- **Published:** Harvard University Press, 2017
- **Main argument:** The very concept of a unified "Muslim world" that rose and declined is a 19th-century construction, jointly produced by European imperialists and pan-Islamist reformers.

## 7. The Madrasa-as-Anti-Science Framing

### The Standard Claim (Huff, Makdisi-derived):
George Makdisi's *The Rise of Colleges* (1981) documented that madrasas were legally endowed for Islamic law. Huff and others extrapolated: madrasas = only religious sciences = no room for natural science = institutional failure.

### Counter-evidence:

**F. Jamil Ragep (2005)**
- **Title:** "Copernicus and His Islamic Predecessors: Some Historical Remarks"
- **Published:** *History of Science*, 43, 2005
- **Main finding:** Demonstrates that madrasa-based scholars did cutting-edge astronomy, directly contradicting Huff's institutional argument.

**Sonja Brentjes (2018)**
- **Title:** *Teaching and Learning the Sciences in Islamicate Societies (800-1700)*
- **Published:** Brepols, 2018
- **Main argument:** Comprehensive survey showing that mathematical sciences were taught in, around, and through madrasas in ways the "excluded from madrasas" narrative entirely misses.

**İhsan Fazlıoğlu (various)**
- Turkish historian of science who has documented extensive scientific teaching in Ottoman madrasas, including mathematics, astronomy, and natural philosophy.

**Asad Ahmed & Robert Wisnovsky (various)**
- Have documented that post-classical Islamic philosophy and logic were *standard madrasa subjects*, not excluded ones.

## 8. Key Methodological Critiques of "Decline" Studies

The quantitative decline studies (Chaney especially) face several critiques:

1. **Source bias:** Biographical dictionaries (*tabaqat*) were compiled by and for religious scholars. Using them to count scientists is like using a directory of churches to measure the secular population.

2. **Category error:** The "scientist" vs. "religious scholar" binary is anachronistic. Many scholars did both. Ibn al-Haytham was devout. Nasir al-Din al-Tusi ran an observatory and wrote theology.

3. **Geographic selection:** Studies focused on the Arab heartland miss that the center of gravity of Islamic science shifted — to Iran, Central Asia, India, and the Ottoman Empire — rather than disappearing.

4. **Benchmark problem:** "Decline" relative to what? Relative to Europe's post-1600 acceleration? That's a different claim than "religion killed science."

5. **Manuscript gap:** Hundreds of thousands of Arabic, Persian, Turkish, and Urdu scientific manuscripts remain uncatalogued or unstudied. Claims about "decline" are based on incomplete evidence.
